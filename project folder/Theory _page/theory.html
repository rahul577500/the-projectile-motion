Projectile Motion - Theory

1. Introduction

Projectile motion is a form of motion experienced by an object (called a projectile) that is launched into the air and moves along a curved path under the sole influence of gravity.

2. Key Characteristics

  • Two-Dimensional Motion: Motion occurs in both horizontal and vertical directions.
  • Parabolic Trajectory: The path followed by the projectile is always a parabola (assuming air resistance is negligible).
  • Independence of Motion: Horizontal motion does not affect vertical motion and vice-versa.
  • Acceleration: The only acceleration acting is gravity (g = 9.81 m/s² on Earth), which acts vertically downward.

3. Motion Components

To analyze projectile motion, the initial velocity u at launch angle θ is split into two components:

Horizontal Component
ux = u cosθ
Remains constant throughout the flight
Vertical Component
uy = u sinθ
Changes continuously due to gravity

4. Equations Used in the Project

Horizontal displacement:
x = ux × t
Vertical displacement:
y = uyt − ½gt²
Vertical velocity at time t:
vy = uy − gt

5. Important Calculations

Time of Flight (T):
T = 2u sinθ / g
Maximum Height (H):
H = u² sin²θ / 2g
Horizontal Range (R):
R = u² sin2θ / g

6. Important Observations

  • Maximum Range: Achieved when the launch angle is 45°.
  • Complementary Angles: Angles like 30° and 60° produce the same range but different heights.
  • Velocity at Peak: At maximum height, vertical velocity becomes zero while horizontal velocity remains constant.

7. Real-World Applications

  • Sports: Throwing a ball, kicking a football, shooting a basketball.
  • Nature: Water sprayed from fountains or garden hoses.
  • Technology: Artillery shells, missiles, and space launch analysis.

8. Gravity Values Used in Simulation

Earth: g = 9.81 m/s²
Moon: g = 1.62 m/s²
Mars: g = 3.71 m/s²
